Moving to Philadelphia: A Data Scientist's Take

Personal · 4 min read · March 2025

I moved to Philadelphia in early 2025. I didn't know the city well. I had visited once, briefly, and left with impressions of cheesesteaks and a surprisingly good art museum. Everything else I had to figure out by arriving.

There's a particular feeling that comes from being new to a place — a low-grade data-hunger. You're constantly collecting observations and trying to build a coherent model of how things work: which neighborhoods are walkable, which coffee shops are actually quiet in the morning, where the good running paths are. It's exploratory data analysis, except the dataset is your daily life.

The Routine-Building Phase

The first thing I did was establish anchors — fixed points in the week that gave structure before the rest filled in. A consistent grocery run. A regular run through Clark Park on Sunday mornings. A coffee shop close enough to walk to, good enough to want to. These aren't exciting discoveries, but they're load-bearing. Without them, new cities have a way of feeling formless.

I noticed something worth noting: the habits I managed to establish in the first three weeks became permanent. The ones I kept telling myself I'd start "once things settled" largely didn't happen. There's a small-sample lesson in there about the value of early signal — strong patterns established in noisy conditions tend to persist.

Finding Community Without a Campus

Moving for a job in your mid-twenties is different from moving for school. You don't arrive with a built-in cohort. There's no orientation week, no shared syllabus. The mechanisms of adult friendship — repeated proximity and low-stakes interaction over time — have to be constructed deliberately.

I've been more intentional about this than I expected to be. I joined a running group that meets on Saturday mornings. I've been to a few data community meetups. Neither of these is a silver bullet, but they're the closest adult equivalent of a class you see twice a week — regular, repeated contact with people who share at least one interest.

What Philadelphia Actually Is

Philadelphia is a walking city in a way I didn't expect. The grid makes navigation intuitive, and the neighborhoods are distinct enough to be interesting without being precious about it. It's also, quietly, a city that rewards noticing: the mural arts program means that a mundane block can have a remarkable wall. The farmers' markets are genuinely excellent. The transit is imperfect but functional.

I'm still building my model of this place. But the initial evidence is encouraging, and I'm learning to treat that as meaningful even in small sample sizes.
